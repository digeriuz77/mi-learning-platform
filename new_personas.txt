Persona: “Daniel Ortiz”

Basic Profile

· Age: 37

· Occupation: Automotive technician at a mid-sized repair shop

· Location: Suburban New Jersey

Background & Family Situation

Daniel grew up in a working-class family. His father was a truck driver and smoked heavily; his mother worked part-time as a receptionist. Smoking was normalized in his home, and he started experimenting at 15.

Now, Daniel lives with his partner of 10 years, Melissa (35), and their two kids, Liam (8) and Ava (5). Melissa does not smoke and has been gently encouraging him to cut back for years, mainly because of the kids’ exposure and his persistent morning cough.

Lifestyle & Daily Routine

· Wakes up at 6:30 AM, smokes his first cigarette with coffee in the garage.

· Works 8 AM–5 PM. The shop culture includes smoke breaks—most of his coworkers smoke, and breaks often function as social downtime.

· Smokes around 10–12 cigarettes per day, more on stressful days.

· Goes to the gym once a week with Melissa but often feels winded quickly.

· Evenings are spent cooking dinner, helping with homework, and watching TV to unwind—often accompanied by another cigarette outside on the porch.

· Weekends include family outings; he tends to smoke more if drinking beer during social gatherings.

Motivation to Change (Intrinsic & Extrinsic)

Intrinsic motivations:

· Concern about long-term health—his father developed COPD, which scared him.

· Wants to be more active with his kids; he gets winded playing soccer with his son.

· Feels guilty about exposing his family to secondhand smoke or smelling like smoke around the kids.

Extrinsic motivations:

· Melissa’s encouragement and her worry about his health.

· The rising cost of cigarettes—he notices how much it adds up each month.

· Workplace starting to push for a smoke-free environment.

· Doctor recently suggested he may be developing early signs of hypertension.

Reasons to Change (Change Talk)

· “I want to be around for my kids as they grow up.”

· “I’m tired of this morning cough—it makes me feel older than I am.”

· “I hate feeling controlled by a habit.”

· “I’d like to save money for a family vacation.”

· “I miss feeling fit.”

Reasons Not to Change (Sustain Talk)

· Smoking helps him cope with stress at work—especially difficult customers.

· He fears weight gain; tried quitting once and gained 12 pounds.

· Social aspect—smoke breaks are when he bonds with coworkers.

· Worry that quitting will make him irritable around his kids.

· He believes he’s “not strong-willed enough” because previous attempts didn’t last.

· Cigarettes feel like his one reliable way to decompress.

Stage of Change

He is in the contemplation stage: genuinely considering quitting but ambivalent, unsure he can succeed, and still finds comfort in smoking.

Persona: “Aisha Khan”

Basic Profile

· Age: 24

· Occupation: Graduate student in social work

· Location: Minneapolis, Minnesota

Background & Family Situation

Aisha is the eldest daughter in a Pakistani-American family. Her parents immigrated to the U.S. before she was born. Her family is close-knit and values health, education, and community involvement.

Aisha started smoking socially during her undergraduate years, mainly to fit in with a friend group. Over time, it evolved into a coping mechanism for academic stress and anxiety. Her family does not know she smokes—they would strongly disapprove—and she hides it carefully.

She lives in a shared apartment with two roommates, both of whom vape but don’t smoke cigarettes.

Lifestyle & Daily Routine

· Attends graduate classes three days a week; interns at a community mental health clinic part-time.

· Smokes 4–6 cigarettes per day—usually outside the apartment building or while walking to class.

· Drinks coffee constantly and often pairs it with an early-morning cigarette before campus.

· Practices yoga twice a week but often feels self-conscious about the smell of smoke on her clothes.

· Uses smoking as a treat after completing stressful assignments.

· Avoids smoking around anyone from her family or cultural community.

Motivation to Change (Intrinsic & Extrinsic)

Intrinsic motivations:

· Hates feeling like a “hypocrite” as a future social worker advising clients on healthy coping strategies.

· Wants to manage stress and anxiety in healthier ways.

· Feels physically sluggish, especially during yoga or winter walks.

· Worried about long-term health, especially after an aunt was diagnosed with lung cancer.

Extrinsic motivations:

· Doesn't want her family to find out and be disappointed.

· The cost of cigarettes is difficult on a grad-student budget.

· Roommates complain about the smell on her jackets.

· Wants to enter her internship placement without worrying about smelling like smoke.

Reasons to Change (Change Talk)

· “I want to live by the values I’m planning to teach my clients.”

· “My body feels better when I take even short breaks from smoking.”

· “I’m tired of hiding this part of my life.”

· “I can’t afford cigarettes on top of tuition and rent.”

· “I want to feel strong and healthy.”

Reasons Not to Change (Sustain Talk)

· Smoking helps her manage anxiety, especially during exam periods.

· Offers a quick break from overwhelming emotions.

· Feels like smoking gives her a moment of solitude in a busy life.

· Nervous that if she quits, she'll lose her main coping tool without having another one in place.

· Worries she’ll struggle socially if her friends smoke or vape around her.

Stage of Change

Aisha is in the preparation stage: she is seriously considering quitting soon and has begun looking into stress-reduction strategies, but hasn’t chosen a quit date yet.

Persona: “Margaret ‘Maggie’ Reynolds”

Basic Profile

· Age: 62

· Occupation: Part-time librarian (recently reduced hours)

· Location: Rural Oregon

Background & Family Situation

Maggie has been smoking since she was 18. She grew up in a time when smoking was common and socially accepted, especially among women seeking independence. Most of her adult life—college, marriage, parenting, career—has included smoking as a constant.

She is widowed; her husband passed away five years ago from a heart attack (he was also a smoker). Maggie lives alone in a small house she’s owned for 30 years. She has two adult children:

· Emily (38), lives out of state and frequently urges her mother to quit

· Ben (35), lives nearby but avoids the topic to prevent conflict

Maggie occasionally watches her two young grandchildren on weekends.

Lifestyle & Daily Routine

· Smokes 15–18 cigarettes per day, mostly at home.

· First cigarette within 10 minutes of waking, paired with tea and the morning news.

· Limited physical activity due to arthritis in her knees.

· Evenings are quiet—reading, gardening in warmer months, watching TV. Smoking is woven into all these routines.

· Social circle is small; smoking is often her companion during long periods alone.

· Has tried quitting twice in the past, once in her 40s and once after her husband died—both attempts lasted less than a month.

Motivation to Change

Intrinsic motivations:

· Wants to be present and capable as a grandmother—able to get on the floor and play.

· Increasing shortness of breath and chronic bronchitis worry her.

· Feels a growing sense of loss of control and dependence.

· Doesn’t like the smell lingering in her home and clothes.

Extrinsic motivations:

· Doctor recently warned her that continued smoking will worsen her arthritis and lung health.

· Her daughter’s persistent concern and fear of “losing her too.”

· Grandchildren have commented on the smell of smoke.

· Rising healthcare costs on a fixed income.

Reasons to Change (Change Talk)

· “I don’t want smoking to be the reason I can’t enjoy my grandchildren.”

· “I’ve lost enough already—I don’t want to lose my health too.”

· “I hate being dependent on something.”

· “I want my house to feel clean again.”

· “I know my body would thank me.”

Reasons Not to Change (Distinct Barriers / Sustain Talk)

· Strong belief that “the damage is already done”—questions whether quitting at her age matters.

· Smoking is deeply tied to grief and loneliness; fears quitting will make evenings unbearable.

· Skeptical of quitting aids—had side effects from nicotine patches before.

· Fear of failure: “I don’t want to try again and feel like a disappointment.”

· Smoking feels like her last personal comfort after many life losses.

· Change feels exhausting; she worries she doesn’t have the energy for another attempt.

Stage of Change

Maggie is in the ambivalent contemplation stage—emotionally conflicted, aware of the risks, but uncertain whether change is worth the effort at this point in her life.

Persona: “Mark Thompson”

Basic Profile

· Age: 46

· Occupation: Regional sales manager for a national food and beverage supplier

· Location: Greater Manchester, England

Background & Family Situation

Mark grew up in a working-class household in the North of England, where meals were hearty, filling, and an important way of showing care. Food was rarely discussed in terms of calories or health—it was about comfort, value, and togetherness.

He is married to Sarah (44), a secondary school administrator. They have two children:

· Ella (17), studying for A-levels

· Josh (14), keen on gaming and football (mostly watching rather than playing)

Sarah worries about Mark’s health but avoids raising it directly after previous conversations led to defensiveness and jokes to shut the topic down.

Lifestyle & Daily Routine

· Spends most weekdays driving between clients across the North West.

· Often skips breakfast or grabs a bacon butty and a sugary coffee from a petrol station.

· Lunch is usually a meal deal, Greggs, or client lunches at pubs.

· Dinners are late and generous; evening meals are the main family time.

· Drinks 2–4 pints most evenings to “switch off.”

· Sedentary workday; averages 3,000–4,000 steps/day.

· Current weight: ~19½ stone (≈124 kg), height 5’10”.

· Has tried Slimming World, calorie tracking apps, and “cutting carbs,” but hasn’t maintained results.

Motivation to Change

Intrinsic motivations:

· Feels constantly tired and uncomfortable in his body.

· Joint pain (knees and lower back), poor sleep, and loud snoring.

· Avoids photos and feels self-conscious in clothes.

· Misses feeling capable and energetic.

Extrinsic motivations:

· GP recently flagged pre-diabetes and rising blood pressure.

· Daughter asked why he doesn’t walk the dog with her anymore.

· Work trousers feeling tight; had to size up.

· Aware of increased risk of heart disease, which runs in his family.

Reasons to Change (Change Talk)

· “I don’t want to be knackered all the time.”

· “I want to be around and healthy as the kids become adults.”

· “I don’t fancy ending up on medication if I can avoid it.”

· “I’d like to feel comfortable in my own skin again.”

· “I’m fed up with starting again every few months.”

Reasons Not to Change (Sustain Talk / Barriers)

· Food and drink are his main ways of relaxing and socialising.

· Work feels too busy and unpredictable to plan meals or exercise.

· Pub culture is tied to both work and friendships.

· Fear that weight loss means giving up all enjoyment.

· Previous attempts make him doubt he can do it long term.

· Uses humour and minimising (“It’s just middle age”) to avoid discomfort.

Emotional & Psychological Context

· Uses food and alcohol to cope with stress and pressure to provide.

· Tends toward all-or-nothing thinking: strict dieting followed by giving up.

· Feels shame after overeating, which reinforces the cycle.

· Values independence and resists being told what to do—even by healthcare professionals.

Stage of Change
Mark is in the contemplation stage, edging toward preparation. He recognises the health risks but feels overwhelmed and unsure how to change without making life miserable.

She is married to Rahim (42), a minicab driver with long, irregular hours. They have three children:

· Main meal is late evening, typically rice, curry, fried snacks, and roti.

· Frequently snacks while cooking, often without noticing.

· Drinks sweet tea several times a day.

· Limited structured exercise; feels uncomfortable attending mixed-gender gyms.

· Current weight: ≈95 kg (15 stone), height 5’4”.

· Has tried fasting, online diet plans, and “eating less,” but struggles to sustain changes.

Intrinsic motivations:

· Feels exhausted and achy, especially knees and lower back.

· Struggles to keep up with her youngest child.

· Wants to feel confident and comfortable in her clothes.

· Feels frustrated by lack of time and energy for herself.

Extrinsic motivations:

· GP raised concerns about gestational diabetes history and current risk of Type 2 diabetes.

· Family history of diabetes and heart disease.

· Wants to set a healthy example for her children—especially her daughter.

· Difficulty finding modest clothing that fits comfortably.

· “I don’t want diabetes like my mum.”

· “I want to feel lighter and have more energy.”

· “I want my kids to see me looking after myself.”

· Fear of family members questioning or undermining changes.

Emotional & Psychological Context

· Carries mental load of caregiving and household management.

· Feels pressure to meet cultural and family expectations.

· Experiences shame and self-blame after overeating.

· Wants change but feels stuck between personal needs and family roles.

Stage of Change

Nadia is in the contemplation stage, with moments of preparation. She is aware of health risks but unsure how to make changes that feel respectful, realistic, and sustainable.

Persona: “Tom Walker”

Basic Profile

· Age: 52

· Gender: Male

· Ethnicity: White British

· Occupation: Facilities manager at a large secondary school

· Location: Nottinghamshire, England

Stage of Change

Preparation moving into early Action Tom has decided he wants to lose weight and has already started making changes, but his confidence is fragile and he’s worried about maintaining momentum.

Background & Family Situation

Tom has been overweight for most of his adult life. His weight increased steadily after his late 30s as work became more sedentary and family responsibilities grew.

He is married to Helen (50), a teaching assistant. They have one adult daughter who recently moved out. With fewer family demands, Tom has more time—but also feels a sense of emptiness and loss of routine.

Lifestyle & Daily Routine

· Works early shifts (6:30 AM start), active during the day but inconsistent.

· Previously relied on crisps, biscuits, and canteen food.

· Current weight: ≈17 stone (108 kg), height 5’9”.

· Over the past 3 weeks, he has:

o Cut down sugary snacks

o Started walking 20–30 minutes most evenings

o Reduced portion sizes at dinner

· Weighs himself daily and feels anxious about fluctuations.

· Has lost about 5 lbs, but worries it’s “just water weight.”

Motivation to Change

Intrinsic motivations:

· Wants to feel physically capable and less stiff.

· Enjoys the early benefits—better sleep, improved mood.

· Feels proud he has actually “started” this time.

Extrinsic motivations:

· GP referred him to an NHS weight-management programme.

· Brother recently had a heart scare.

· Upcoming family holiday—wants to feel comfortable walking and swimming.

Reasons to Continue Changing (Strengthening Change Talk)

· “I feel better already—I don’t want to lose that.”

· “This feels different because I started small.”

· “I want to prove to myself I can stick with something.”

· “I don’t want to end up on statins if I can help it.”

New Barriers & Sustain Talk (Specific to This Stage)

· Fear of slipping up and “ruining it.”

· Anxiety when the scales don’t move.

· Finds planning tiring after a long workday.

· Worried walking won’t be “enough” to make a real difference.

· Old habits resurface during stress or tiredness.

· Sensitive to comments from others (“You’ll be back to normal soon”).

Emotional & Psychological Context

· Motivated but vigilant and self-critical.
· Equates success with weight loss rather than behaviour change.

· Prone to catastrophising small setbacks.

· Needs reassurance without being rescued or lectured.

What He Needs From MI at This Stage

· Reinforcement of self-efficacy

· Normalising lapses and plateaus

· Shifting focus from outcomes to behaviours and values

· Helping him define his own markers of success

· Supporting autonomy: “This is your plan, not a programme’s.”

MI-Relevant Client Statements

· “I don’t want to get my hopes up too much.”

· “I’ve done well, but it could all fall apart.”

· “I’m trying not to be obsessed… but I kind of am.”

· “I think I can do this—if I don’t mess it up.”
